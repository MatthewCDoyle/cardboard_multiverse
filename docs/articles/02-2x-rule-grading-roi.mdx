## The rule that saves more money than any other in the hobby

Most collectors submit cards because it feels right — they think the card is nice, they believe the player is good, and they assume grading will add value. Sometimes it does. Often it doesn't.

The 2x Rule is a financial discipline that removes the feeling from the calculation. If the math works at 2x or better, you have meaningful upside and real downside protection. If the math doesn't work, the submission is a gamble, not an investment.

---

## The formula

```
ROI Ratio = PSA 10 Sale Price ÷ Total Cost In

Total Cost In = Raw Card Price + Grading Fee + Shipping (both ways) + Insurance
```

**Threshold:** ROI Ratio must be ≥ 2.0 to proceed.

At a 2.0 ratio, you double your total invested cost if you receive a PSA 10. This provides margin to absorb one of two scenarios: (1) you receive a PSA 9 instead of a 10, or (2) the market moves slightly against you between submission and receipt.

---

## Working example: Mahomes 2017 Prizm Silver RC

**Step 1: Pull the PSA 10 comp**
Search "Mahomes 2017 Prizm Silver RC PSA 10" in eBay sold listings. Recent sales: $4,100–$4,300. Use the conservative end: **$4,100**.

**Step 2: Determine raw card cost**
You find a raw copy at a card show. The seller is asking $150. After negotiation: **$130**.

**Step 3: PSA grading fee**
At the standard Economy tier ($50 per card for cards valued $499 and under — *confirm current PSA fee schedule as fees change*): **$50**.

*Note: Higher-value cards may require Express or Walkthrough submission tiers at $150–$300+. Always check the current PSA fee schedule for the appropriate tier.*

**Step 4: Shipping and insurance**
Round trip shipping to PSA: approximately $30. Insurance on a potential $4,100 card: approximately $15. Total: **$45**.

**Step 5: Calculate total cost in**
$130 (raw) + $50 (grading) + $45 (shipping/insurance) = **$225 total cost in**

**Step 6: Calculate the ratio**
$4,100 ÷ $225 = **18.2**

This submission passes the 2x Rule with massive room to spare. Even if you receive a PSA 9 ($480), you still recover $480 on $225 invested — a 2.1x ratio. Even the downside scenario barely breaks the threshold.

---

## Working example where the math fails: budget modern card

**Card:** A mid-tier 2020 Prizm RC of a promising but unconfirmed player

**PSA 10 comp:** $85  
**Raw card cost:** $22  
**PSA grading fee:** $50  
**Shipping/insurance:** $30  
**Total cost in:** $102  

**ROI Ratio:** $85 ÷ $102 = **0.83**

This is an immediate pass. You cannot make money submitting this card even if you receive a perfect PSA 10. The grading fee alone exceeds the PSA 10 value.

This scenario plays out constantly with bulk modern submissions — collectors submitting $20–$30 raw cards at standard grading fees, paying more in fees than the card can ever recover.

## The three-outcome model

When you submit a card, you get one of three outcomes. Before submitting, calculate the expected value of each:

| Outcome | Probability (estimate) | Sale Price | Net Profit |
|---|---|---|---|
| PSA 10 | Based on your condition assessment | $4,100 | $4,100 - $225 = $3,875 |
| PSA 9 | Remaining probability | $480 | $480 - $225 = $255 |
| PSA 8 or below | Low but possible | ~$180 | $180 - $225 = -$45 |

Weight these by your honest probability estimate. If you think the card has a 60% chance of a 10, 35% chance of a 9, and 5% chance of an 8: expected value = (0.60 × $3,875) + (0.35 × $255) + (0.05 × -$45) = $2,325 + $89 - $2 = **$2,412 expected net profit**.

That is a compelling expected value. The 2x Rule is a shortcut that approximates this calculation — if the 2x threshold is met, the three-outcome model almost always produces a positive expected value even with conservative probability weights.
