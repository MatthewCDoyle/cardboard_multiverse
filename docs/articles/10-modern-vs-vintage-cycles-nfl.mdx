## The cycle that most collectors don't see until it's too late

In 2020 and 2021, the sports card market experienced one of its most dramatic expansions in history — driven by pandemic-era stimulus spending, a captive audience with no live sports, and social media amplification of card values. Joe Burrow's 2020 Prizm Silver RC in PSA 10 hit $1,200. The market felt like it would never stop.

By 2023, that same card traded for $180. An 85% decline from peak.

Joe Montana's 1981 Topps RC in PSA 10 went from roughly $90,000 at the 2021 peak to around $68,000 at the 2024 trough. A 24% decline from peak.

Both cards dropped. The vintage card dropped by about a quarter. The modern card dropped by seven-eighths. This is not coincidence — it is the structural difference between vintage condition-scarce assets and modern print-abundant speculation plays.

---

## Why modern cards amplify market cycles

**High gem rates mean high supply.** When 40–65% of submitted modern Prizm cards grade PSA 10, the supply of PSA 10s is not fixed. Every pack opened and graded creates new supply. In bull markets, mass grading adds supply faster than demand grows. When demand contracts, the supply overhang becomes visible.

**Print runs are large and known.** Modern hobby boxes typically carry disclosed or estimable print run information. The total possible supply is calculable. This limits how high any valuation can go before it becomes demonstrably unreasonable.

**The buyer base is speculative, not collector-driven.** Modern card buyers include a large proportion of investors who entered during the 2020–2021 cycle and will exit at the first sign of trouble. This creates herding dynamics — fast run-ups and fast crashes.

---

## Why vintage dampens market cycles

**Condition scarcity is fixed.** The population of 1981 Topps Montana PSA 10s is not going to increase meaningfully. This sets a structural floor that ultra-modern cards don't have.

**The buyer base is collector-weighted.** Vintage buyers tend to be long-term collectors with emotional attachment to the players and eras they're collecting. This reduces panic selling in downturns.

**Liquidity is lower — which cuts both ways.** Vintage cards don't have thousands of comparable sales a month. This means prices move slowly in both directions. The downside protection is real; so is the limitation on the upside.

---

## The 4-to-6-year macro cycle

Historically, the card market peaks on a roughly 4-to-6-year cycle, driven by:

- Recession and expansion economic cycles
- Generational nostalgia peaks (when the cohort that grew up watching a player enters peak earning years)
- Major cultural events (retirement, death, Hall of Fame induction)

Modern cards peak and trough within the same overall cycle but with 3–4x the amplitude. A modern card that falls 80% in a market correction might return 400% in the next bull market — but you have to survive the drawdown.

**The practical framework:**

Buy vintage when the overall market is hot and vintage is receiving "rising tide" attention. The incremental downside in a vintage star's PSA 10 is managed.

Buy ultra-modern at the *trough* of market cycles — the 12–18 months after a peak, when speculative buyers have exited and collector-investors dominate the supply. The Burrow example: buyers in mid-2023 at $180 were back above $600 by late 2024 when his team returned to Super Bowl contention.

Knowing where you are in the cycle is the single most valuable skill in the hobby.
