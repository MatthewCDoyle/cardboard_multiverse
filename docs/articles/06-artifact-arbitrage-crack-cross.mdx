## Why a BGS 9.5 and a PSA 10 can contain the same card

Beckett Grading Services (BGS) and PSA grade on different standards. BGS grades on four subgrades — Centering, Corners, Edges, and Surface — each scored individually, with the final grade derived from the lowest subgrade. A BGS 9.5 requires all four subgrades to be 9.5 or better, with no subgrade below 9.

PSA grades holistically — a single overall grade with no published subgrades. PSA's 10 standard requires near-perfect centering, sharp corners, clean edges, and pristine surface. But PSA's tolerance is generally understood to be slightly more lenient than BGS's in practice.

The result: a BGS 9.5 card frequently meets PSA 10 standards. But it trades as a BGS 9.5 — often at a significant discount to the PSA 10 equivalent.

## The anatomy of a crack-and-cross opportunity

**Step 1: Find the spread**

For any target card, pull:
- Recent BGS 9.5 sale price
- Recent PSA 10 sale price

The spread is your gross opportunity. Example using a modern basketball RC:
- BGS 9.5: $280
- PSA 10: $520
- Gross spread: $240

**Step 2: Check the subgrades**

A BGS 9.5 is only a crack-and-cross candidate if all four subgrades are 9.5. Many BGS 9.5s achieve that overall grade with uneven subgrades — for example: Centering 9.5, Corners 9.5, Edges 9, Surface 9.5. This is a composite 9.5 but the Edges subgrade of 9 signals a flaw that PSA will likely grade 9 or below.

**Only target BGS 9.5 Black Labels (all four subgrades 9.5) or BGS 9.5s where the listing photos show the subgrade breakdown and all are 9.5 or higher.**

Any BGS 9.5 with a subgrade below 9.5 is not a reliable crack-and-cross candidate. Move on.

**Step 3: Calculate total cost**

- BGS 9.5 purchase price: $280
- Cracking fee (if you use a service): $10–$20
- PSA submission fee (appropriate tier): $50
- Round-trip shipping and insurance: $40
- Total cost in: **$390**

**Step 4: Calculate net profit**

- Expected PSA 10 sale price: $520
- Total cost in: $390
- Net profit if PSA 10: **$130**
- ROI: $520 ÷ $390 = **1.33x**

This particular example barely clears the 1.3x threshold — marginal at best. For crack-and-cross to be worth the execution risk, aim for a minimum 1.5x ratio, ideally 2.0x. The execution risk — receiving a PSA 9 instead of 10 — is real and needs to be priced in.

## When crack-and-cross works best

**At market dips.** BGS 9.5 prices of a given card often trade at a larger discount to PSA 10 when the market is soft — because PSA 10 buyers maintain their price floor while BGS 9.5 sellers discount to move inventory. Executing crack-and-cross during market dips captures the maximum spread.

**On cards with large PSA/BGS premium spreads.** Some cards have structural 2x or greater spreads between BGS 9.5 and PSA 10 — particularly cards of transcendent players where PSA 10 collector demand is intense and BGS 9.5 demand is softer. Research the spread history before committing.

**With SGC 10s as well.** SGC's 10 grade (Pristine) often contains PSA 10-quality cards at similar discounts. The same methodology applies — check subgrades or condition evidence before purchasing, calculate the spread, and execute only when the math works.

---

## The risk you must accept

You will receive PSA 9s on some percentage of crack-and-cross submissions. Even a BGS 9.5 Black Label with perfect subgrades will occasionally receive a PSA 9. Factor a 15–25% failure rate into your expected value calculation. If the expected value is still positive after accounting for 9-outcomes, the opportunity is viable. If it is not, pass.

The crack-and-cross strategy rewards discipline and patience — waiting for the right spread at the right market moment — not volume and speed.
