## What gem rate actually measures

Gem rate is the percentage of all graded copies of a given card that receive the highest grade — PSA 10 (or BGS 9.5 Black Label equivalent). It is one of the most useful single numbers in evaluating a card's scarcity profile, and most collectors either don't know it or don't apply it correctly.

Here is the core principle: **a PSA 10 is only as scarce as the gem rate makes it.**

If 64% of Wembanyama's 2023 Prizm Silver copies achieve PSA 10, then a PSA 10 is essentially the standard result of submitting a well-kept copy. It is not scarce. It is expected.

If 2.24% of Jordan's 1988 Fleer copies achieve PSA 10, then a PSA 10 represents an extraordinary outcome. Out of every 100 copies submitted, only 2 receive the top grade. That is genuine condition scarcity.

---

## The numbers side by side

| | Wembanyama 2023 Prizm Silver | Jordan 1988-89 Fleer |
|---|---|---|
| Total PSA graded | ~12,000 | ~18,400 |
| PSA 10 copies | ~7,680 | ~413 |
| Gem rate | ~64% | ~2.24% |
| PSA 10 price | ~$380 | ~$4,800 |

The Jordan has a PSA 10 that is roughly 18x more expensive than the Wembanyama, and the condition rarity explains a significant portion of that premium. Even setting aside player legacy and era, there are simply far fewer Jordan PSA 10s available relative to the demand for them.

---

## What this means for the risk profile

**Modern ultra-chrome cards (Prizm, Chrome, Optic) grade easily.** The manufacturing precision of current Fanatics/Panini product — consistent centering tolerances, surface protection, quality control — means that well-kept raw copies routinely grade PSA 10. This is great for collectors who want clean product. It means condition scarcity is not a significant driver of value for modern PSA 10s. The value driver is the player, the narrative, and the production print run.

**Vintage cards grade hard.** The variables that made 1988-89 Fleer production condition-sensitive — paper stock, printing tolerances, wax pack staining, 35+ years of circulation — cannot be replicated. Every PSA 10 Jordan from 1988 exists because a specific physical card survived specific circumstances. That is non-reproducible scarcity.

**This does not make modern cards worse investments.** It makes them *different* investments with different risk drivers. A Wembanyama Prizm PSA 10's value will be driven almost entirely by Wembanyama's career performance, not by supply constraints. If he becomes a generational all-time player, the card's value climbs. If his career disappoints, 7,680 PSA 10s seeking fewer buyers is a supply problem.

A Jordan 1988 Fleer PSA 10's value is already anchored by fixed supply, confirmed cultural legacy, and 35 years of appreciation history. The downside scenarios are narrower.

---

## The practical framework

When evaluating any card purchase, ask two questions before checking the player:

1. What is the gem rate?
2. What is the total PSA 10 population?

A gem rate above 30% means the PSA 10 is abundant. The card's value is a pure player bet. A gem rate below 5% means condition scarcity is a structural floor in the valuation. You are buying both the player and the rarity.

Neither is inherently better — they just require different investment theses. Know which one you're making.
