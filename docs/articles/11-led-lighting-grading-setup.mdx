## Why the light source changes everything

A card that looks near-mint under incandescent room lighting often reveals significant surface wear under a direct LED. Hairline scratches on chrome surfaces, print lines on cardstock, faint wax staining on vintage — all of these are invisible under soft ambient light and clearly visible under a direct, angled LED source.

Professional graders at PSA evaluate every card under strong, direct lighting. When you evaluate under your living room lamp and they evaluate under professional grade LEDs, you are not looking at the same card. Setting up a proper lighting station closes that gap.

---

## The equipment (affordable and effective)

**Primary: Daylight LED desk lamp**
You need a lamp with a color temperature of 5000–6500K (labeled "Daylight" on the box). This temperature closely approximates natural sunlight and reveals surface conditions accurately. Standard "warm white" bulbs (2700–3000K) add a yellow cast that hides surface defects.

Cost: $20–$40 for a quality adjustable desk lamp. This is the single most important piece of equipment in the setup.

**Secondary: Small LED flashlight**
A compact LED flashlight (not UV — standard white LED) at roughly 200 lumens gives you a concentrated, directional light source for edge and corner inspection. Hold it parallel to the card surface and roll it slowly to catch defects.

Cost: $8–$15.

**Optional: UV/Black light flashlight**
For vintage cards (pre-1995), UV light reveals staining, restorations, and alterations that are completely invisible under white light. A PSA-9-looking vintage card under normal light can show obvious staining under UV. Essential for serious vintage evaluation.

Cost: $12–$20.

**Card handling: Tongs or cotton gloves**
Never handle cards with bare fingers during evaluation. Skin oils transfer to surfaces — especially chrome and foil cards — and can create the very defects you're trying to avoid. Card tongs are preferable; cotton gloves are acceptable.

---

## The four-step evaluation sequence

**1. Centering — neutral overhead light**

Before using directional light, evaluate centering under even overhead illumination. Hold the card at arm's length and visually assess left/right and top/bottom border balance. For a more precise measurement, place the card face-up and measure border widths with a metal ruler.

PSA 10 requires 55/45 or better. Anything worse than 60/40 is a hard nograde.

**2. Corners — raking LED light**

Position your desk lamp so the light hits the card surface at a shallow angle (roughly 20–30 degrees from parallel). This "raking" light casts shadows from surface irregularities that would be invisible under direct overhead light.

Examine each corner individually. Look for: rounding (soft corner), creasing (bent corner), fraying (chipped corner). Rotate the card so each corner faces the light source directly.

**3. Edges — LED flashlight along the edge plane**

Hold the card vertically and position your LED flashlight so the beam travels along the card's edge from left to right. Look along the edge plane, not at the card face. Chips, nicks, and dings appear as dark interruptions in the edge profile.

Repeat for all four edges. A single edge chip, however small, is a PSA 10 disqualifier.

**4. Surface — 45-degree angled LED**

This is the most revealing check and the one most collectors skip. Hold the card at a 45-degree angle to your primary LED lamp. Slowly rotate the card while keeping it at this angle. Watch for:

- Hairline scratches: thin bright lines crossing the surface
- Print lines: faint parallel marks from the printing process
- Wax staining (vintage): dull, slightly cloudy patches
- Dimpling: tiny indentations from pressure

A surface that looks pristine under normal light may show 3–4 hairline scratches under this technique. These scratches are visible to PSA graders and will cost you a grade.

## Surface characteristics by card type

**Prizm and Chrome (modern):** The reflective chrome surface scratches readily and shows every contact mark under LED. Common sources of scratches: pack-searching friction, sleeve insertion/removal, and any contact with other cards in a box. Always pull Prizm from packs directly into a sleeve.

**Vintage cardstock (pre-1990):** The semi-gloss surface shows print defects and handling marks differently than chrome. Under raking light, look specifically for creasing — even micro-creasing from a card being slightly bent is visible and will affect the grade.

**Foil surfaces (SP, Finest, Chrome refractors):** Foil surfaces hide some defects and reveal others. Under UV light, foil cards sometimes show manufacturing artifacts that affect grading. The 1993 SP Derek Jeter foil, for example, is notorious for surface defects visible only under specific lighting angles.
