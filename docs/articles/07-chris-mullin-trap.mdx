## The trap that catches most new collectors

New collectors learn that Hall of Famers are safe. The logic seems airtight: Hall of Fame status is permanent, great players are always collectible, and the designation filters out the noise of speculation.

The logic is wrong.

The Hall of Fame has 200+ members. A significant portion of their rookie cards have been flat to declining in real-dollar terms for a decade. The Hall of Fame tells you a player was objectively great. It tells you nothing about whether the broader culture cares enough to drive sustained card demand.

---

## The case studies

**Chris Mullin — 1987-88 Fleer RC PSA 10: ~$380**

Five-time All-Star. Olympic gold medalist on the 1992 Dream Team. First Ballot Hall of Famer. Fleer RC in a condition-sensitive set. And yet, the card trades for $380 — a number that has barely moved in years.

Mullin played his entire career in Golden State before they were interesting nationally. He had no controversy, no memorable media moments, no cultural presence beyond the sport. His buyer pool is primarily older collectors who watched him play, with limited generational refresh.

**Mitch Richmond — 1989-90 Fleer RC PSA 10: ~$140**

Six-time All-Star. Hall of Famer. Had the misfortune of spending the prime of his career in Sacramento, one of the NBA's most commercially invisible markets. His card has almost no trading volume, which means price discovery is slow and liquidity is poor.

**Gary Payton — 1991-92 Fleer RC PSA 10: ~$95**

One of the greatest defensive players in NBA history. Nine-time All-Defensive First Team. Hall of Famer. His card trades for $95 in PSA 10. The Glove was exceptional. He is not culturally transcendent.

---

## The five-question test

Before allocating capital to any player's cards, run this test:

1. **Does someone who doesn't watch basketball know their name?** If no, you are buying a collectors' card, not a cultural artifact.
2. **Is there a memorable narrative arc?** Drama, comeback, controversy, or legacy-defining moment? Mullin, Richmond, and Payton all lack one.
3. **Is there active new-generation discovery?** YouTube channels, social media accounts, or media coverage actively introducing this player to under-30 collectors? If not, the buyer pool is aging, not growing.
4. **Did they play in a major market at their peak?** Market size is not the whole story — Jordan is from Chicago — but playing in Sacramento vs. Los Angeles affects the size of the original collector base and continues to affect demand.
5. **Is there a cultural hook beyond basketball?** Endorsements, personality, media presence, a memorable phrase or moment that lives on in broader culture?

Five no's means you are buying a collectible with a ceiling. One or two yes answers gives you a card with appreciation potential. Three or more yes answers and you're approaching transcendence territory.

---

## The allocation rule

Use the five-question test to determine how much of your portfolio you're willing to put into a player position. At CMD, we suggest: if a player scores 0–1 on the five-question test, treat it as a hobby card — buy for personal enjoyment at a price you're comfortable holding forever. Do not treat it as an investment position.

This rule alone would save most collectors from the most common trap in the hobby.
