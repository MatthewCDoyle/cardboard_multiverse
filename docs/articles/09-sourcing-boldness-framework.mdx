## Why the best inventory isn't listed

The secondary card market — eBay, COMC, card shows — represents a fraction of the total inventory in circulation. The rest sits in personal collections, storage units, basements, and garages. Owners range from casual collectors who bought cards in the 1980s and forgot about them, to estates where the family has no idea what the collection contains, to card show veterans who have more in the van than on the table.

The collectors and dealers who access this unlisted inventory consistently outperform those who only buy from the visible market. The difference is not knowledge — it is the willingness to have a slightly uncomfortable conversation that most people avoid.

That willingness is the Sourcing Boldness Framework.

---

## The two sourcing environments and their signals

**Estate sales** are one of the most reliable environments for finding undervalued card collections. The estate executor's goal is to liquidate everything quickly, not to maximize value on any individual category. Cards from the 1980s and 1990s are routinely underpriced because the executor used outdated price guides or simply guessed.

**Signals at estate sales:**
- Cards on a table with round-number pricing ($5 each, $20 a box)
- Mixed sports and eras in the same box — indicates a casual collector, not a specialist
- Box sets still factory sealed from the early 1990s
- Any mention of "my grandfather" or "my uncle" as the original collector

**Card shows** are a different environment. Here, the inventory on the table is intentionally displayed. The hidden inventory is what the dealer didn't bother to set up — because they ran out of table space, because they haven't priced it yet, or because they're waiting for the right buyer to ask.

**Signals at card shows:**
- A dealer who has clearly brought more than fits on the table (boxes on the floor, overfull binders)
- Long-time dealers who've been in the hobby 20+ years — they accumulate inventory faster than they display it
- Any mention of "I've got more in the car/van/storage"

## The one-more-question framework

The framework consists of two questions and one request, asked in sequence.

**Question 1: "Is this everything you have available?"**

This is the opener. Most sellers — at estate sales or card shows — will either say yes (correct answer, move on) or hesitate (the signal you're looking for). A pause, a "well...," or a sideways glance almost always means there is more. Hesitation is inventory.

**Question 2: "What was the collection originally focused on — any particular sport, era, or player?"**

This question does two things. First, it signals that you know what you're talking about. Casual sellers are more comfortable dealing with someone who speaks the hobby's language. Second, it helps you calibrate whether the unlisted inventory is worth pursuing. A "mostly 1990s basketball" answer is exciting. "Mostly 2005–2010 basketball commons" is less so.

**The request: "I'd love to take a look at whatever else you have if it's not too much trouble."**

The word "trouble" is deliberate. It acknowledges the seller's time and removes the pressure of obligation. Most sellers who hesitated at Question 1 will either go get the inventory themselves or direct you to it. The ones who say no are protecting something they know has value — which is actually another useful signal for a second conversation.

---

## Making the offer

When you gain access to unlisted inventory, resist the impulse to negotiate aggressively on the first offer. The relationship value of being the buyer who is easy to deal with is worth more than the margin you'd capture by grinding the price.

**For bulk collections:** Offer a per-card price based on your honest assessment of the collection's average value. If a collection of 500 cards is mostly commons with 20–30 stars, a $0.50–$1.00 per card bulk offer is fair and usually accepted. This signals competence without feeling predatory.

**For specific cards:** If you identify a card you want specifically, ask the seller's price before offering your own. Many sellers will price below market because they don't know the market — and if they know the price is fair to them, the transaction closes easily.

**The cardinal rule:** Never lowball the first offer. A lowball offer at an estate sale ends the relationship and often ends the transaction entirely. A fair offer builds trust and often leads to the seller volunteering additional inventory you hadn't asked about.
