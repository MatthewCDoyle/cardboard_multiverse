## The biggest structural shift since the junk wax era

In the early 1990s, the sports card market exploded — and then collapsed — because too many companies produced too many cards with no scarcity discipline. The industry spent a decade recovering.

The 2026 Fanatics licensing transition is the mirror image: a *consolidation* event, not an explosion. One company is acquiring the exclusive rights to produce licensed NBA, NFL, and MLB cards. The era of Panini Prizm, Select, Mosaic, and Optic as we know them ends when those licenses expire.

For collectors who understand what this means, the current moment is a window. For those who don't, it will look like chaos.

---

## What exactly is changing

**Panini's NBA license expires.** Panini has held the exclusive NBA trading card license for over a decade, producing Prizm, Select, Hoops, Mosaic, Optic, and numerous other sets. When the license transfers to Fanatics, Panini can no longer produce NBA-licensed cards.

**The Prizm brand as you know it may not continue.** Fanatics will introduce their own brand identity. The Prizm name, the Silver parallel format, the established parallel hierarchy — all of this is Panini IP. The new Fanatics product may be superior, inferior, or simply different. It will not be "Prizm" in the way that word currently means something to collectors.

**The last era of Panini Prizm becomes finite.** Every 2024-25 Panini Prizm Basketball card in existence will be the last of its kind. That finiteness will be priced in eventually — the question is whether it happens before or after the transition.

---

## The three scenarios collectors are discussing

**Scenario 1: Fanatics products are well-received.** The market transitions smoothly, Fanatics introduces a compelling product, and the Panini vintage becomes a dated nostalgia category — still collected, but not at a significant premium to current prices. This is the bear case for Panini hold positions.

**Scenario 2: Fanatics products are poorly received in early years.** Quality control issues, unfamiliar branding, or design missteps in the first 1–2 years of Fanatics product create a quality gap. Collectors flock back to Panini product in the secondary market, driving prices up. This is the bull case.

**Scenario 3: Market uncertainty creates a dip before recovery.** The transition period itself creates price depression as collectors wait and see. Buyers who act during the uncertainty period — acquiring last-era Panini product at temporarily depressed prices — capture the upside when clarity returns.

Historical precedent from licensing transitions in other categories suggests Scenario 3 is most likely, with elements of Scenario 2 in the first 12–18 months of new product.

---

## What to do right now

**If you own Panini Prizm PSA 10s of confirmed stars:** Hold. The finite-era narrative will strengthen as the transition approaches, not weaken.

**If you've been considering raw Panini Prizm acquisitions:** The next 6 months represent a lower-attention window. Once the transition is imminent, the "last era" narrative will be everywhere and prices will reflect it.

**For sealed product:** Last-year-of-production sealed hobby boxes from major sports have a track record of appreciation over 5–7 year windows. If sealed product is in your model, this is the last cycle of Panini NBA product.

**For Fanatics product:** Wait for at least one full production cycle before allocating significant capital. Let the product quality and market reception establish before committing.

The transition is coming regardless of what individual collectors do. The question is whether you're positioned in front of it or behind it.
