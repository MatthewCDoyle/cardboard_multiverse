## Why most collectors make bad buying decisions

Bad buying decisions in the hobby share one common feature: they happen without checking. Someone sees a card, recognizes the player, feels the pull, and buys — without knowing the current market price, without evaluating the condition honestly, and without running the financial math.

The 5-Minute Evaluation is a discipline, not a tool. It is a forced pause between the emotional response to seeing a card and the financial decision to buy it.

---

## Step 1: Pull the eBay comps (90 seconds)

Open eBay on your phone. In the search bar, type the card's key identifiers in this order:

`[Player Name] [Year] [Brand] [Set] [Card Type]`

Example: `Mahomes 2017 Prizm Silver RC`

Filter to **Sold listings** (not active listings — active prices are asking prices, not actual sales). Find the 3 most recent sales that match your card's grade level.

Write down three numbers:
- Recent PSA 10 sale price
- Recent PSA 9 sale price
- Recent raw (ungraded) sale price for comparable condition

If you can't find comps in 90 seconds, either the card has low sales volume (a liquidity warning) or your search terms are off. Adjust and retry.

---

## Step 2: Note the spread (15 seconds)

Calculate the PSA 9-to-10 spread. If PSA 10 = $1,800 and PSA 9 = $200, the PSA 9 Penalty is extreme — receiving a 9 means getting approximately 11% of the PSA 10 value.

This number tells you how much grade uncertainty matters. A tight spread ($400 vs $280) means a 9 is acceptable. A wide spread ($1,800 vs $200) means you need high confidence in a 10 or you should buy raw.

---

## Step 3: Condition assessment (2 minutes)

Examine the card in this order:

**Centering first.** Hold the card at arm's length. Does it look visually centered? A card that looks off-center to the naked eye will grade 8 or below. Prizm and Chrome: PSA uses a 55/45 threshold for a 10. If you're unsure, measure with a centering tool.

**Corners second.** Turn the card to catch light. Look at each corner with the light raking across the surface. Soft corners (slightly rounded) grade 9. Bent or creased corners grade 8 or below. Four sharp, point-tight corners are required for a 10.

**Edges third.** Roll the card gently in your fingers. Feel for chips along the white border edge. Chipped or dinged edges kill a 10 grade immediately.

**Surface last.** Hold the card under an angle of light. Look for scratches, print lines, wax staining, or creases. Chrome surfaces scratch easily — hairline scratches visible under light are common PSA 9 killers.

**Assign a grade estimate:** Be honest. If you think it's a 9, it is probably an 8.5. Graders are harsher than self-evaluation.

---

## Step 4: Run the 2x Rule (30 seconds)

The formula: `PSA 10 sale price ÷ (raw card price + grading fee) ≥ 2.0`

If the PSA 10 sells for $400 and the raw card would cost you $80 and grading costs $25, your ratio is $400 ÷ $105 = 3.8. The math works.

If the PSA 10 sells for $140 and the raw costs $65 and grading costs $25, your ratio is $140 ÷ $90 = 1.55. The math does not work — even getting a PSA 10 leaves you with minimal profit, and any lower grade loses money.

---

## Step 5: Make the decision (15 seconds)

- If 2x math works AND condition looks like a 10 → **buy to grade**
- If 2x math works but condition is uncertain → **buy raw, sell raw**
- If 2x math doesn't work → **pass, or buy for personal collection at a price you're happy holding**

The discipline is in the pass. The fastest way to accumulate capital in the hobby is to not buy cards that don't pass the test.
