## Why this set is different from everything that came after

The 1986-87 Fleer Basketball set was produced at a time when card companies were not thinking about the secondary market. There was no grading industry. No population reports. No concept of gem rate. Cards were made to be handled, sorted, rubber-banded, and stored in shoeboxes.

The paper stock was soft. The centering tolerances were loose by modern standards. The printing registration was imprecise. And the wax packs that housed them left staining on the surfaces of cards adjacent to the wax.

All of this means that finding a well-centered, corner-sharp, surface-clean example of almost any card in this set is genuinely difficult. For the key cards, it approaches impossible.

---

## The production characteristics to know

Before examining individual cards, understand what you're grading against:

**Centering:** Fleer's 1986 production notoriously ran off-center on a significant percentage of the print run. A well-centered copy of any key card commands a meaningful premium over an off-center example at the same nominal grade. PSA's centering tolerance for a 10 is 55/45 or better — many raw copies of this set don't come close.

**Surface:** The card stock is semi-gloss, which shows print defects and handling marks readily. Wax staining from pack storage affects perhaps 20–30% of surviving copies at some level. Under LED lighting, surfaces that look clean to the naked eye often reveal micro-scratching.

**Corners:** The card corners are particularly vulnerable. This set's corners are not as sharp as modern chrome-based cards, which makes maintaining four mint corners through 40 years of circulation genuinely rare.

---

## Tier 1: The anchor cards (PSA 10 gem rate under 1%)

**#57 — Michael Jordan RC**
The anchor of the entire hobby. With roughly 9,600 graded copies and a gem rate around 0.4%, there are approximately 38 PSA 10 examples in existence. Recent sales in PSA 10 range from $28,000 to $45,000 for standard copies, with premium-centering examples achieving higher. In PSA 9, the card trades between $1,400 and $2,200.

*Condition note:* Centering is the primary killer. The Jordan RC runs notoriously left-heavy on many print sheets. A well-centered example with clean surfaces is the holy grail of the modern hobby.

**#26 — Charles Barkley RC**
Barkley's transcendent personality keeps this card in permanent demand. PSA 10 examples trade around $1,100 — more accessible than Jordan but still condition-sensitive. Gem rate sits below 2%.

**#82 — Hakeem Olajuwon RC**
Underappreciated. Olajuwon's global recognition has grown significantly, and his PSA 10 RC ($900–$1,400) is one of the better values in the set relative to his historical ranking.

---

## Tier 2: The Dream Team anchors

**#53 — Patrick Ewing RC**, **#68 — Karl Malone RC**, **#27 — Clyde Drexler RC**, **#36 — Joe Dumars RC**

These cards represent the backbone of an 80s collecting position. None approach Jordan's cultural weight, but all are genuine Hall of Famers with stable long-term demand. PSA 10s range from $300 to $700. Malone's card is particularly condition-sensitive due to its border design.

---

## Tier 3: The legacy legends (already-established RCs not in this set)

**#109 — Magic Johnson**, **#8 — Larry Bird**
Both Magic and Bird had rookie cards in earlier sets — their 1986 Fleer cards are second-year issues. Still significant, still condition-sensitive, and still priced at meaningful premiums in PSA 10 ($400–$900 range).

---

## The sticker set: overlooked condition rarity

The 11-card sticker set issued with the base set is frequently overlooked by new collectors. The stickers are if anything *more* condition-sensitive than the base cards — the sticker backing creates a different surface that grades harshly, and the Jordan sticker (#8) in PSA 10 is rarer on a population basis than many collectors realize.

---

## Building the set: a strategy

For the completist: prioritize the Jordan RC first as the anchor, then work outward through Barkley, Olajuwon, and Ewing. Mid-range grade copies of the commons can often be acquired at low per-card costs through dealer lots — focus your PSA 10 budget on the key cards.

For the investor: the Jordan RC in PSA 8 offers the best liquidity-to-cost ratio. PSA 9s carry the 9 Penalty in this set — the spread between 9 and 10 is significant enough that a 9 has historically underperformed both the 8 and the 10 as a hold.
