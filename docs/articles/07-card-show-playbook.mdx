## The table that sells itself

The best card show booths have a specific quality: buyers feel comfortable exploring without a seller hovering. They find prices without asking. They discover unexpected value in the bargain boxes. They linger, which means they spend more.

The worst booths are the opposite: unpriced inventory, a seller who quotes every card from memory while the buyer waits, and nothing accessible below $20. Buyers browse for two minutes and move on.

Setup determines most of your revenue before the show opens. Here's how to set it up right.

---

## The three-tier inventory split

Divide your show inventory into three categories before you pack the car:

**Tier 1 — Showcase:** Your 10–20 key assets. PSA 10 graded cards of stars, low-pop vintage, anything above $50. These go in the display case at eye level. Price each one individually with a clearly visible sticker or tag.

**Tier 2 — Color-coded boxes:** Your velocity inventory. Modern commons, mid-grade raw, team lots, and anything priced $1–$10. These go in color-coded bins where the color signals the price tier — buyers self-serve without asking.

**Tier 3 — Binder:** Your personal collection overflow, overstock, and cards you're willing to deal on but not actively promoting. Keep this under the table. Serious collectors will ask — and that's exactly who you want to have a binder conversation with.

---

## The color-coded bargain box system

The most effective friction-removal tool at any card show is a clearly signposted price box system. Use four colors with consistent, permanent pricing:

- **Red box: $1 each** — commons, duplicates, anything you want to move at volume
- **Blue box: $3 each** — better commons, mid-tier players, team fillers
- **Yellow box: $5 each** — solid cards with active buyer bases, short prints
- **Green box: $10 each** — lower-tier stars, recent rookies, fan favorites

The critical discipline: **restock each box aggressively throughout the show.** An empty bargain box is dead real estate. When a red box runs low, rotate stock from your binder or bring out new supply. Buyers who see a full box dig. Buyers who see a sparse box assume the good cards are gone.

Never merge price tiers into one box. Mixed pricing kills the self-service behavior — buyers stop and ask for every card instead of digging freely.

---

## Showcase rotation: the rule that most sellers miss

When a showcase card sells, you have a decision: leave the slot empty or fill it immediately.

**Fill it immediately.** An empty display case slot signals to browsers that the good cards are gone. It dampens the psychological energy of the booth. Have your next-tier cards already sorted and sleeved before the show opens, so you can rotate a sold card out and a replacement in within 60 seconds.

The rotation order: when a Tier 1 card sells, move a strong Tier 2 card into the showcase. Restock the box from binder inventory. The table always looks full and active.

---

## Pricing showcase cards at shows

Show prices should sit at 85–95% of recent eBay comps, not at or above them. Buyers at shows are paying a premium for immediacy and the ability to examine the card in person — that experience is worth something, but it does not justify eBay pricing plus eBay fees. The show buyer is effectively eliminating your eBay fees and your shipping hassle. Pass part of that savings to them in the price.

Cards priced at eBay retail at a show sit all day. Cards priced at a visible discount move in the first two hours.

---

## The one-more-question framework

When a buyer lingers on a specific category — asks about multiple basketball cards, picks up and replaces three different Prizm cards — they are telling you something. They want more of what they're looking at.

The one-more-question: *"I have more of those in the back — what players or sets are you looking for specifically?"*

This question has unlocked more sales than any other single phrase in the card show environment. Buyers who are deep in a niche will always tell you what they want if you ask directly. And most sellers never ask.

Have your binder organized by sport and player so you can pull relevant cards within 60 seconds of asking. A buyer who asked the question is already primed to buy what you find.
