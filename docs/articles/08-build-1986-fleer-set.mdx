## The right way to approach set building

Most collectors approach the 1986-87 Fleer set backwards — they fixate on the Jordan RC, spend most of their budget there, and then realize they have 131 remaining cards to acquire with whatever's left. This leads to a half-finished set that sits incomplete for years.

The better approach: build the foundation first, then work toward the anchor card. A complete low-grade raw set is worth more as a coherent collection than a single graded Jordan surrounded by nothing.

---

## Step 1: Define your format goal before buying anything

The set looks very different depending on your goal:

**Raw complete set (lowest cost):** Acquire all 132 base cards plus 11 stickers in raw condition, tolerating mid-grade quality for commons and near-mint for key cards. Total cost at current market: approximately $800–$1,500 depending on Jordan raw condition.

**PSA-graded complete set:** Grade every card to PSA standards. This is a multi-year, multi-thousand-dollar project. The Jordan RC alone at PSA 8 runs $600+. Not recommended for budget collectors — set a 5-year horizon if this is the goal.

**Hybrid approach (recommended):** Acquire commons raw in bulk, grade only the key cards (Jordan, Barkley, Olajuwon, Ewing, Malone at minimum). This balances cost, completion speed, and long-term value.

---

## Step 2: Source commons as a lot

The single most efficient way to build the base is buying a partial or near-complete raw lot from a single seller. Search eBay for "1986 Fleer Basketball lot" and "1986-87 Fleer Basketball complete raw set." Dealers who've acquired entire collections often sell the commons as lots at $0.50–$2.00 per card.

When you receive the lot:
- Sort against your checklist immediately
- Identify which key cards are in the lot
- Evaluate each key card under LED light for condition
- Separate potential-graders from raw-holders

**Never pay single-card prices for commons.** The non-star cards in this set (non-All-Stars, bench players of the era) have minimal individual value. Paying $5–$10 per common individually when lots move at $0.75 per card is a budget mistake.

---

## Step 3: The key card acquisition sequence

Once your commons are sourced, acquire the key cards in this order:

**Priority 1: Jordan RC (#57)**
This is the unavoidable anchor cost. Set your budget for the Jordan first — everything else is sized around it. For raw: a mid-grade (VG-EX) raw copy runs $400–$800. For PSA 8: $600–$900. For PSA 9: $1,500–$2,200. Do not buy the Jordan under time pressure — wait for a well-photographed copy at the right price.

**Priority 2: Barkley RC (#7), Olajuwon RC (#82)**
Both are accessible at $30–$100 raw depending on condition. Acquire raw first, evaluate for grading potential with the 2x Rule.

**Priority 3: Ewing RC (#32), Malone RC (#68), Drexler RC (#26)**
The second tier of Hall of Famers. Raw copies run $15–$50. Complete this tier before looking at single-card upgrades anywhere else.

**Priority 4: Magic Johnson (#53), Larry Bird (#8)**
Second-year cards for both legends (their rookies are in earlier sets). Still condition-sensitive and worth acquiring in solid condition.

**Priority 5: Complete the remaining commons**
Fill gaps from your lot acquisition. The final 20–30 commons are often the hardest to find cheaply because individual sellers price them higher than bulk.

---

## The sticker set: a separate track

The 11-card sticker set (separate from the 132-card base set) features the same key players but grades differently — the sticker backing creates surface challenges and the sticker itself must be in perfect shape to grade well. The Jordan sticker (#8) has a surprisingly low PSA 10 population.

Build the sticker set as a separate acquisition. Source a complete raw sticker set first ($50–$150 for a mid-grade raw complete set), then evaluate individual stickers for grading potential. Do not intermix sticker acquisition budget with base set budget — they are separate completion goals.
