## The grade that feels close but isn't

PSA grades run from 1 to 10. PSA 9 is described as "Mint" — an exceptional grade. In practice, for modern ultra-chrome cards of key players, receiving a PSA 9 instead of a PSA 10 is often a financial catastrophe.

Mahomes 2017 Prizm Silver RC: PSA 10 trades at $4,200. PSA 9 trades at $480. That is an 88% discount for one grade point.

Luka Doncic 2018-19 Prizm Silver RC: PSA 10 at $1,800. PSA 9 at $220. An 88% discount.

These are not edge cases. For modern Prizm and Chrome cards of transcendent players, the PSA 9 Penalty — the value lost by not achieving a 10 — is structural and predictable.

---

## Why the spread is so extreme

**The 10 is expected, not exceptional.** When 40–65% of submitted modern Prizm cards achieve PSA 10, the market does not reward a 9 as "close to perfect." It treats a 9 as a card that failed to achieve what was achievable. The 9 represents a card with a flaw — a centering issue, a surface scratch, a ding — that prevented the expected outcome.

**The buyer pool bifurcates completely.** PSA 10 buyers of key modern cards are often collectors or investors who specifically want the top grade. They will not substitute a 9. The PSA 9 buyer pool is entirely different — and smaller, because the 9 offers neither the prestige of the 10 nor the low cost of the raw card.

**Liquidity on 9s is poor.** Modern PSA 9s of key cards sit in eBay listings for months. The PSA 10 of the same card typically sells within days at a fixed price. Carrying a PSA 9 means carrying illiquidity along with the value discount.

---

## The cards most affected by the 9 Penalty

The penalty is most severe on:

1. **Modern Prizm silver base RCs of top-tier stars.** The combination of high gem rates, strong PSA 10 demand, and binary collector preference creates maximum spread.
2. **Any card where the PSA 10 population is under 2,000 copies.** Tighter supply amplifies the premium.
3. **Cards of players with "transcendent" status.** For Mahomes, LeBron, Jordan in any modern Prizm release — the premium buyer only wants the top.

The penalty is *less severe* on vintage cards. A 1986 Fleer Jordan PSA 9 trades at $1,500–$2,200 — a discount to the PSA 10, but not an 88% one. Because the PSA 10 is genuinely scarce in vintage, the PSA 9 is treated as the high-grade alternative, not a defective product.

---

## How to avoid the trap

**Rule 1: Never buy a PSA 9 of a modern key card with investment intent.** Buy the PSA 10 or buy raw. The PSA 9 is neither.

**Rule 2: Before submitting raw cards for grading, apply the 2x Rule.** If your PSA 10 upside is not at least 2x your raw cost plus grading fee, do not submit. If you receive a 9, you will likely lose money.

**Rule 3: Evaluate centering before submission.** For Prizm and Chrome cards, centering is the most common killer of PSA 10 grades. A card that is off-center by more than the PSA tolerance will grade 9 at best, often 8. This is visible to the naked eye with a ruler or centering tool. Do not submit cards where the centering is in doubt.

**Rule 4: Understand the nograde strategy.** Cards that will not achieve a 10 are better sold raw than graded. A raw Mahomes Prizm with visible centering issues can sell for $80–$120 raw. Graded as a PSA 9, it sells for $480 — but you spent $25–$50 on grading plus submission time. The raw economics often favor liquidating nogrades immediately.
